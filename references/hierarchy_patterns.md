# Hierarchy Pattern System

## Purpose

This document defines the hierarchy behavior system
for AI-native presentation generation.

The objective is NOT:
visual decoration.

The objective IS:
controlled cognitive attention guidance.

Hierarchy determines:

- what the audience notices first
- what feels important
- how interpretation flows
- how cognitive pressure is controlled

Strong hierarchy creates:

clarity,
focus,
and
communication stability.

---

# Core Philosophy

High-level presentations are NOT:
collections of equally important elements.

Strong presentations establish:

visual dominance.

Hierarchy should guide:

attention
before
interpretation.

The audience should never ask:

“What should I look at first?”

---

# Global Hierarchy Principle

Every slide must establish:

Primary
↓
Secondary
↓
Supporting

Hierarchy should remain:

- visually obvious
- cognitively stable
- compositionally intentional

Avoid:

- equal-weight composition
- visual competition
- flat information structure
- fragmented attention systems

---

# Dominant Element Principle

Each slide must contain:

one dominant element.

The dominant element may be:

- mechanism figure
- conceptual diagram
- key scientific finding
- academic message
- comparison structure
- teaching illustration

The dominant element should:

- attract attention immediately
- occupy the strongest visual position
- control slide interpretation

Avoid:

- multiple dominant objects
- equally large visual blocks
- competing visual anchors

---

# Visual Weight Principle

Hierarchy is controlled through:

- size
- contrast
- spacing
- position
- density
- color restraint

Higher importance should receive:

- larger scale
- stronger spacing isolation
- clearer positioning
- cleaner surrounding space

Avoid:

- increasing importance only with color
- decorative emphasis effects
- excessive bold styling

---

# Spatial Hierarchy Principle

Position creates meaning.

Preferred hierarchy positioning:

- upper-left
- central dominant region
- strong directional flow

Important elements should occupy:

stable,
intentional,
and visually anchored positions.

Avoid:

- floating layouts
- unstable alignment
- scattered positioning logic

---

# Whitespace Hierarchy Principle

Whitespace strengthens hierarchy.

More important elements should receive:

more breathing space.

Whitespace communicates:

- significance
- confidence
- visual authority

Avoid:

- equal spacing everywhere
- compressed dominant visuals
- crowded focal regions

Rule:

Important content should breathe more.

---

# Typography Hierarchy Principle

Typography should establish:

clear cognitive layers.

Preferred hierarchy:

Title
↓
Core takeaway
↓
Supporting interpretation
↓
Annotation/details

Typography hierarchy should feel:

stable,
editorial,
and intentional.

Avoid:

- oversized decorative headers
- aggressive bold usage
- inconsistent scaling systems
- visually noisy text structures

---

# Density Hierarchy Principle

Hierarchy is also controlled by density.

Important areas should feel:

cleaner,
clearer,
and more isolated.

Supporting information may carry:

higher local density.

Avoid:

uniform density across the slide.

Uniform density destroys:

attention guidance.

---

# Contrast Hierarchy Principle

Contrast should support:

attention routing.

Contrast may be created through:

- scale
- whitespace
- positioning
- density
- tonal restraint

Avoid:

- excessive color contrast
- aggressive visual effects
- decorative highlighting

Strong hierarchy feels:

calm,
not loud.

---

# Figure Hierarchy Principle

Figures should behave according to:

communication importance.

## Dominant Figures

Should:

- occupy major visual space
- anchor reading flow
- guide interpretation

Preferred for:

- mechanisms
- frameworks
- teaching diagrams
- core evidence

---

## Supporting Figures

Should:

- remain visually secondary
- support interpretation
- avoid competing with dominant visuals

Avoid:

multiple equally dominant charts.

---

# Annotation Hierarchy Principle

Annotations should remain:

supportive,
not dominant.

Preferred annotation behavior:

- directional
- concise
- visually restrained

Annotations should clarify:

relationships
and
interpretation.

Avoid:

- paragraph annotations
- oversized labels
- annotation saturation

---

# Slide-Type Hierarchy Patterns

## Cover Slide

Dominance:

visual atmosphere
or
core identity.

Behavior:

- large focal area
- minimal text
- strong breathing space

---

## Teaching Slide

Dominance:

visual explanation.

Behavior:

diagram
→ explanation
→ takeaway

---

## Scientific Mechanism Slide

Dominance:

mechanism figure.

Behavior:

mechanism
→ evidence
→ interpretation

---

## Faculty Interview Slide

Dominance:

academic message.

Behavior:

message
→ framework
→ supporting evidence

---

## Comparison Slide

Dominance:

contrast relationship.

Behavior:

comparison structure
→ interpretation
→ implication

Avoid:

equal visual chaos between sides.

---

## Summary Slide

Dominance:

core takeaway.

Behavior:

minimal density
→ strong clarity
→ memorable structure

---

# Reading Hierarchy Principle

Slides should guide:

eye movement naturally.

Preferred reading path:

Title
→ dominant visual
→ takeaway
→ supporting information

Avoid:

- scattered reading order
- disconnected information islands
- unstable visual jumps

Eye movement should feel:

smooth,
guided,
and cognitively efficient.

---

# Multi-Layer Hierarchy Principle

Complex slides may contain:

primary hierarchy
and
local hierarchy.

Example:

Slide level:
mechanism dominates.

Inside mechanism:
process sequence dominates.

Hierarchy should remain:

nested,
stable,
and interpretable.

Avoid:

hierarchy collapse.

---

# Scientific Hierarchy Behavior

Scientific presentations require:

evidence hierarchy.

Preferred scientific structure:

Mechanism
↓
Evidence
↓
Interpretation
↓
Implication

Avoid:

equal-weight data dumping.

---

# Teaching Hierarchy Behavior

Teaching presentations require:

cognitive guidance hierarchy.

Preferred structure:

Concept
↓
Visual explanation
↓
Example
↓
Key takeaway

Avoid:

simultaneous multi-concept competition.

---

# Faculty Interview Hierarchy Behavior

Faculty presentations require:

strategic professionalism hierarchy.

Preferred structure:

Academic positioning
↓
Research framework
↓
Evidence support
↓
Future potential

Avoid:

publication dumping
and
achievement overload.

---

# Rhythm Hierarchy Principle

Hierarchy should vary across slides.

Not every slide requires:

maximum intensity.

Presentations should alternate between:

- high-focus slides
- breathing slides
- transition slides
- summary slides

Strong pacing creates:

cognitive rhythm.

---

# Anti-Flatness Principle

Flat layouts destroy:

attention control.

Avoid:

- equal card sizes
- identical visual blocks
- repeated grid monotony
- uniform typography scaling

Slides should contain:

intentional imbalance.

Controlled asymmetry often improves:

hierarchy
and
visual maturity.

---

# Anti-AI-Slop Hierarchy Rules

Strictly avoid:

- generic card grids
- equal-weight infographic systems
- random icon repetition
- template-style hierarchy
- startup dashboard aesthetics

Hierarchy should feel:

editorial,
scientific,
and cognitively intentional.

---

# Hierarchy Stability Principle

Hierarchy systems should remain:

- reusable
- scenario-adaptive
- visually stable
- cognitively predictable

The objective is NOT:
maximum visual novelty.

The objective IS:
stable communication quality.

---

# Final Validation

Before finalizing:

Check:

- Is the dominant element immediately obvious?
- Is visual weight properly distributed?
- Does whitespace strengthen importance?
- Is reading order stable?
- Does hierarchy reduce cognitive pressure?
- Does the slide avoid flat composition?
- Does the layout feel editorially mature?

If NO:

rebalance hierarchy.

---

# Final Philosophy

Hierarchy is NOT:
visual styling.

Hierarchy IS:

cognitive attention engineering.
